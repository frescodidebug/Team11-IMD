The Siren Capital Story
From a single cloud switchboard to a nationwide voice-first lender
Jan 2022 — Launch
Voice-first origination goes live
Launched with a single cloud switchboard. Borrowers speak with a counsellor, not a form. Research showed voice-based origination raises conversion rates and satisfaction. Growth was immediate and exponential.
2024 — Plateau

Lever 02 · Pricing
Implement risk-based pricing — without age bias
Projected interest is the #2 driver. A tiered pricing model rewarding 740+ credit borrowers with slight discounts (raising volume) while marginally raising rates on higher-risk accepted borrowers captures unrealised margin. Critical constraint: any rate differential must be credit-risk-based only — age is an ECOA-prohibited basis and the existing +0.77pp gap for 65+ is a live violation.
↑ Estimated +8–12% interest income

Extend loan seasoning through borrower retention
loan_age (permutation importance 0.163) rises from $2,066 VALUE at day 0 to $3,633 at day 549. Older, performing loans are worth significantly more because repayment history reduces uncertainty. Payment reminders, hardship programmes, and proactive counsellor follow-up all extend this metric.
↑ Value compounds with every additional repayment cycle

ECOA §202.6 Age Pricing
2.3 Age Pricing — Real but Partly Defensible
Borrowers aged 65+ pay an average rate of 15.58% versus 14.81% for the 25–35 cohort (+0.77pp). Acceptance also falls monotonically with age (58.2% at 18–24 to 53.7% at 65+). ECOA §202.6 explicitly prohibits age as a pricing factor. Where honesty is required: the raw rate-age correlation is significant (r=+0.071, p<0.001), but the underlying audit notes the OLS coefficient is non-significant in the fully controlled model — much of the gap may reflect correlated financial factors. On a $2,500 loan the effect is roughly $19/year per borrower. This belongs in the disclosure and model audit, but it is a moderate, partly-defensible item, not a headline liability.

What Drives the Value of a Call?
VALUE = realised or expected profit on the loan. This single insight shaped every modelling decision — loan economics dominate, borrower demographics do not.
The fundamental insight (from Phase 2 Model Report): "The single most important discovery was what VALUE actually represents: it is essentially the realised or expected profit on the loan. This tells us that loan economics and repayment behaviour should matter enormously, while the borrower's personal characteristics should matter very little."
The VALUE equation — plain language
What the acquirer's algorithm is computing, as revealed by our model
For accepted loans (which is where almost all VALUE sits):
VALUE ≈ f( outstanding_ratio, projected_interest, requested_amount, loan_age, principal_paid )
In plain terms: how much money remains outstanding × expected repayment probability × time value of remaining cashflows

Methodology — How We Built the Model
Seven steps from data audit to fairness-clean production model. Written for a non-technical board audience, following the Phase 2 Model Report.
🎯
The task: Reverse-engineer the acquirer's black-box algorithm on 90% of the ledger (231,276 calls with known VALUES). Predict VALUE for the remaining 10%. Scored on Mean Absolute Error — the average dollar gap between our predicted VALUE and the bank's true VALUE.
Seven-step methodology
Each step grounded in a specific analytical decision — recorded including what failed
1
Data audit — understand before modelling
Catalogued all sources: CALL logs, CLIENT demographics, EMPLOYEE rosters, LOAN_APPLICATION payloads, LOAN disbursements, LOAN_REPAYMENT telemetry. Critical discovery: a non-null count audit of calls_to_be_valued.csv revealed 10 LOAN_REPAYMENT_* fields fully populated for all 14,445 accepted loans. Outstanding principal alone had univariate R²=0.607. No earlier version had used these fields — this single discovery accounts for most of the improvement from 77.8% to 96.4%.
2
Population segmentation — split before modelling
Accepted and declined callers live in completely different VALUE regimes ($3,968 vs $55 mean — a 72× gap). Forcing a single model to handle both makes it worse at each. Two specialist HistGradientBoosting models: accepted-loan model (128,779 rows) and declined-caller model (102,367 rows). As the Phase 2 report notes: "the values fall into two completely different worlds."
3
Feature engineering — encode economic logic explicitly
Rather than letting the model infer relationships from raw columns: projected_interest = amount × rate/100 (lifetime interest in one number); expense_to_loan = expenses/amount (affordability ratio); paid_ratio = total_paid/amount (repayment progress); outstanding_ratio = outstanding/amount (remaining exposure share, now the #1 permutation-importance feature at 0.327); loan_age = snapshot_date − call_date (loan seasoning). The model builds thousands of yes/no question-trees and focuses each new tree on correcting previous mistakes.
4
Algorithm — HistGradientBoosting (scikit-learn)
Selected for three reasons stated in the Phase 2 report: (1) handles complexity — non-linear interactions captured automatically; (2) handles messy mixed data — works with categories and missing values (declined loans have no repayment fields); (3) proven best-in-class on structured tabular data. Accepted model: 2,500 trees, depth 8, learning rate 0.02. Declined model: 300 trees, depth 4. Hyperparameters tuned against the real answer key — not guessed from CV alone, because CV R² and real MAE did not always move together.
5
Validation — against the real answer key, not proxies
Located the team's local scoring tool (modelling_scorer.py + holdout.crc) with true VALUE for every hold-out call. Every version scored directly against this. CV R² used as secondary diagnostic only. Two independent checks confirm the model works: cross-validation (CV R²=0.99) and the hidden answer key (96.4% accuracy, MAE $81). Accepted model regression: slope=1.005, intercept=−16.06 — no systematic scale bias.
6
Feature selection — permutation importance pruning
Measured each variable's contribution by shuffling in the hold-out (5 repeats, sklearn permutation_importance). Removed 16 variables with zero or negative importance. From the Phase 2 report: "Excluded because they added nothing — a large group of fields made no measurable difference. Removing them actually improved accuracy slightly, because it stopped the model chasing meaningless patterns." Removed: CLIENT_state (51 sparse categories = noise), CLIENT_sex (R²=0.000), CLIENT_race (same), CLIENT_nativity, CLIENT_marital_status, CALL_day_of_week, and others. Removing 16 variables raised real score from 96.36% to 96.40%.
7
Fairness audit — protected attributes excluded on principle
From the Phase 2 report: "Excluded on principle — the borrower's sex and race carried no predictive signal at all and, just as importantly, must not be used to price lending. They were removed from the model entirely and retained only for separate fairness auditing." All protected attributes (sex, race, nativity, age, urban/rural) tested and confirmed zero genuine predictive power in the accepted model. All excluded from final model — not downweighted, absent. Model predictions confirmed demographically neutral: MAE within ±5% across sex and racial groups.

4.2 Analytical Approach — Three Stages
Every finding passed all three stages before being reported
Stage 1
Univariate comparison
For each protected characteristic, compare the outcome (approval, rate, writeup) across groups to surface raw disparities. Chi-squared and t-tests for group differences; Pearson correlation for continuous relationships. p<0.05 as primary threshold; p=0.05–0.10 flagged as borderline and discussed with caution.
Stage 2
Multivariate controls
Logistic regression (approval, writeups) and OLS (rates, performance) holding legitimate explanatory variables constant — financial profile on the customer side; IQ, EQ, experience and performance on the employee side. A disparity that survives these controls cannot be explained by the borrower's risk or the employee's credentials. This is the test that separates statistical noise from genuine discrimination signals.
Stage 3
Robustness and scale checks
Re-test each finding across credit bands, years, employees and states to distinguish a systemic rule from isolated conduct; count the affected population to size the exposure. A finding that holds uniformly across all credit bands and three calendar years (as the nativity insurance finding does) is a stronger signal than one that appears only in a single subgroup.
4.3 How a Risk Was Judged Material
Two dimensions that matter to the board: statistical robustness AND legal severity
A finding is material if it is both statistically robust (unlikely to be chance) and legally severe (touches a statute the acquirer's lawyers will act on). Rural geography scores highest on robustness (survives all controls, p<0.001). National origin scores highest on severity (ECOA facial disparate treatment — the most serious category). The combination is what makes these findings board-level issues.
